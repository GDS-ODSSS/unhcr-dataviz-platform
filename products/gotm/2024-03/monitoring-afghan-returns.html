Returning Afghans; voluntarily or deported

In October 2023 a law issued by the Pakistani government caused thousands of Afghans to uproot their lives and cross the border into Afghanistan. Some of these people had been in Pakistan for decades and were in possession of registration documents such as the Proof of Registration Cards, asylum certificates and slips issued by UNHCR. This is just the most recent development in a long history of displacement and return to Afghanistan. According to the data collected by UNHCR on returns, already in 2014, 20% of the Afghan populations were returnees (WB & UNHCR 2016).

Total returns to Afghanistan from 2002 to 2023

Since 2002, UNHCR has facilitated returns of nearly 5.3 million Afghan refugees. To support the return, UNHCR began registering returns including information from and to where Afghans were returning to get a better sense of the support required in return areas. This data on voluntary repatriation allows UNHCR to map out where returns concentrate in time and space posing possible challenges to local absorption capacity.

Who are the returning Afghans?

As these people crossed into Afghanistan, many were met by UNHCR and our partners and provided with assistance but are the needs and characteristics of these households the same as other Afghans. Thanks to an increase in the number of households surveys targeting returning Afghans, we now know that returnees make use of networks and assets acquired while abroad. According to the Afghanistan Living Conditions Surveys in 2013-2014, returnees had higher literacy rates than their counter parts who did not move, with literacy rates amongst Afghans in Pakistan and Iran even higher, likely due to access to national education systems in their country of Asylum. Returnee households also had a lower rate of poverty than other Afghans. UNHCR mapped living conditions of Afghans in a similar fashion in 2023, allowing for comparison with condition pre-the Taliban take over for all Afghan’s including returnees.

In 2019 and ?2022?, UNHCR conducted a survey amongst people in Afghanistan receiving livelihood support, amongst who a large proportion are returnees. Making use of this standardised tool we can visualise how the livelihood opportunities and income changed after the Taliban takeover.

Monitoring media, predicting movements?

That anti-Afghan sentiment contributes to increases in general discrimination leading to premature return or risk of deportation may intuitively make sense but it is hard to prove. How do you measure the impact of a choice of words? Monitoring of social media has allowed us to identify spikes in hateful discourse as well as the prominence of such statements. Hateful speech can be a consequence of high-profile media stories portraying Afghans negatively. Mapping prevalence of hate speech and deportations along the same timeline allows us to examine the link.

Letting the machine do the work: AI-powered analyses of free text information

Returnees needs that differ from other Afghans, needs for shelter and a new livelihood as they may have assets left behind in the country of Asylum. UNHCR uses standardized surveys to determine needs of a population based on predefined categories of needs, but with recent advances in generative artificial intelligence, new opportunities are appearing which may make this work more efficient and effective. During the most recent repatriation plan, UNHCR piloted a tool to automatically capture needs and protection details from a free text description of the household’s situation. The result is a descriptive and thematic analysis of all these conversations. It helps to summarize and prioritize the concerns raised by refugees. In the end it allows UNHCR to better understand what refugees are experiencing and to help.
